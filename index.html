Sixth City — Field Guide

Strategy Approach

Start at thirty-thousand feet. Work your way down. Each step builds context for the next.

The idea is simple: don't start in the weeds. Begin with who the client is and what they need, then zoom in — site structure, page quality, content, links, competition. By the time you're deep in the details, you'll have the full picture.

1
30,000 ft
Foundation

Business Snapshot

First thing after the kickoff call. Create the client's context folder and distill everything you know into a snapshot. This is the lens for every decision that follows.

  • Business basics — what they do, where, how long, size/scale
  • Audience segments — who they serve, what those people care about
  • Core services — the things that make them money
  • Differentiators — why someone picks them over a competitor
  • Competitors — who they see as competition (and who actually is)
  • Current pain points — what prompted them to seek help
Set up the context folder first. Everything downstream — page reviews, content ideas, link targets — maps back to this snapshot.
2
20,000 ft
First impression

Homepage

The homepage is the front door. Someone lands here and within seconds decides whether to stay. Look at it as a first-time visitor, not as an SEO.

  • Information — can you tell what the business does, who it's for, and where?
  • Trust above the fold — proof points, logos, reviews, awards visible without scrolling
  • Conversion setup — hero with CTAs on the left, form or image on the right. Phone number prominent in the header
  • Site sections — does the homepage preview the key areas (services, about, proof)?
  • Clarity over cleverness — is the H1 descriptive or vague?
3
15,000 ft
Site structure

Main Navigation

The nav tells users and search engines what matters most. It should mirror the key services and sections — nothing more, nothing less.

  • Reflects core services — every money-making service should be reachable from the nav
  • Logical grouping — dropdowns that make sense, not kitchen-sink menus
  • No orphans — important pages shouldn't be buried 3+ clicks deep
  • Label clarity — can a stranger understand each link without context?
  • Header CTA — phone number and/or contact button visible at all times
4
8,000 ft
Going deeper

Page Layouts

Now you're inside the key pages — homepage, service pages, industry pages, auxiliary pages. Look at how each one is built.

Hero Sections
  • Left side — H1, supporting description, CTA buttons, proof points / trust signals
  • Right side — relevant image or contact form
  • Impact — does it immediately communicate value and invite action?
Page Templates
  • Section order — are subsections in an effective sequence? (problem → solution → proof → CTA)
  • Content quality — does each section earn its place, or is there filler?
  • CTAs throughout — is there a clear next step on every page, not just in the hero?
  • Consistency — do similar pages follow a consistent template?
Copywriting
  • Audience-first — does it speak to the reader's problems and goals?
  • Keyword signals — natural keyword use in headings, body, and meta?
  • Specificity — concrete details over vague claims?
Images & Media
  • Real and relevant — actual photos of the business, team, and work — not generic stock
  • Support the message — do visuals reinforce what the page is saying?
Internal Links
  • Structural links — nav, footer, sidebar, breadcrumbs all connecting key pages
  • Contextual links — in-content links with descriptive anchor text (not "click here")
  • Money page flow — do supporting pages link to the pages that convert?
5
4,000 ft
Content

Blog

The blog isn't a news feed — every post should serve a strategic purpose. Map each topic to who it's for, where they are in the funnel, and which money page it supports.

  • Audience segment — who is this post written for?
  • Awareness stage — TOFU (problem-aware), MOFU (solution-aware), BOFU (decision-ready)?
  • Money page connection — does this post link to and support a service page?
  • Topic specificity — is it targeted enough to attract the right people?
Watch for generic topics. "What is AI?" attracts everyone and no one. "How AI is changing [specific industry]" attracts the right audience. Specificity wins.
6
2,000 ft
Local businesses only
Local presence

Google Business Profile

For local clients, the GBP is often their most visible asset. It needs to be complete, accurate, and actively maintained.

  • Completeness — all fields filled, correct categories, service areas
  • Reviews — volume, recency, average rating, response rate
  • Posts & updates — are they using posts? How often?
  • Q&A — are common questions answered, or is it empty?
  • Photos — quality images of the business, team, and work
7
1,000 ft
Authority

Backlinks

Get a read on the site's authority. Not just the numbers — understand the quality, relevance, and what gaps need filling to compete.

  • Current profile — domain authority, referring domains, quality distribution
  • Top links — where are the strongest links coming from?
  • Gaps — what do competitors have that this site doesn't?
  • What's needed — realistic link targets given the niche and competition
8
Ground
Competitive intel

North Star Sites

Find competitors who rank well for your priority keywords. These are your reference points — reverse-engineer what's working across every area you've already reviewed.

  • Identify 2–3 top performers — sites that consistently show up for target keywords
  • Structure — how is their site organized? Nav, page hierarchy, URL structure
  • Page quality — hero sections, templates, copy, trust signals
  • Content — blog topics, depth, frequency, funnel mapping
  • Links — internal linking patterns and backlink profile strengths
  • What to steal — patterns and ideas you can adapt (not copy) for the client
These aren't just competitors — they're blueprints. Run them through the same lens you just applied to the client's site. The gaps become your roadmap.
